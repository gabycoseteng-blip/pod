# The Morning Commute — Two-Host Script (LONG / 1.5x edition)
### Friday, July 17, 2026

**Voices:** ALEX = Host A, domain expert (Sulafat). SAM = Host B, sharp generalist (Charon).
**Style (render):** Two polished financial-news anchors — crisp, authoritative, controlled energy, British accent.
**Audience assumption:** fluent in markets and current events. No 101 explainers; lead with the number and the second-order read.
**Segment rules:** open straight into the first story — NO run-of-show / segment preview in the cold open. The meme segment REPORTS the trending meme only — no comedy bit / no performed joke. Close every episode with a ~1 min "One Good Thing": a single uplifting story from anywhere, then the warm sign-off.

> RENDER NOTE: Only `ALEX:` / `SAM:` lines are spoken. `##` headers are silent guides. Hosts announce each segment aloud.

## COLD OPEN

SAM: Good morning, this is The Morning Commute. Friday, July seventeenth. I'm Sam.

ALEX: And I'm Alex. The throughline today: the AI trade just took its second straight down day, and the tell is who's getting punished — the growth names slipping on their guides. Alphabet led it lower on a model delay. Meanwhile the same oil bid that's keeping the Fed boxed in is showing up as real costs everywhere else: a 6-billion-dollar fuel hit one airline just swallowed, a data-center line item landing on ordinary power bills, and a rare-earth chokehold with a trillion-dollar price tag on it. Let's get into it.

## SEGMENT ONE — HEADLINES

SAM: Headlines. World desk first — the Middle East, still setting the tempo for everything downstream.

ALEX: The war's into its second week with no ceasefire in sight, and the market-relevant fact is the chokepoint. Iran's keeping the pressure on shipping through Hormuz, tanker traffic is running thin, insurers are repricing war-risk cover, and crude is holding its bid — Brent up around the mid-80s, near one-month highs, with the market still carrying a tail toward 90 if a tanker or a terminal actually goes.

SAM: And that's the whole macro pivot we've been living in — the war premium that bled out in June is back in the curve.

ALEX: It is, and the point today is the second-order damage, not the headline count. And it isn't just crude — watch the refined products, because that's where households and airlines actually feel it. Diesel and jet-fuel cracks widen when refiners fear feedstock disruption, so the pump price and the airfare can move even before the barrel does. Higher crude flows into corporate fuel bills, into freight and shipping rates, into the Philippine import tab, into a Fed that can't declare victory on inflation. We'll hit all three today. The oil chart is still the single most important input on the board, and it's being set in a strait most listeners will never see.

SAM: And the risk asymmetry from here?

ALEX: Skewed to the upside on price. The spot move has mostly happened; what's cheap is the optionality on a genuine supply disruption. So you don't chase crude here — you own the tail. And every desk is watching the same thing: does OPEC-plus lean in with spare barrels to cap it, or defend price and let it run. That single decision sets the inflation path for the back half of the year.

SAM: Second world story, and it's a leadership shock in Kyiv. Zelensky cleaned house.

ALEX: He dismissed his defense minister, Mykhailo Fedorov, and parliament moved to approve a new wartime government — hundreds turned out to protest in Kyiv. The reporting ties it to a doctrine clash with the army chief, Oleksandr Syrsky: Fedorov was the asymmetric-warfare bet, the drones and the cyber, and Syrsky wants the resources going to holding territory.

SAM: So it's a strategy fight dressed as a personnel move, at a terrible moment to be re-litigating strategy.

ALEX: Exactly — re-arguing doctrine mid-war. The market read is narrow but real: it matters for how durable Western resolve looks into the autumn, and for the European defense-capex theme that's been one of the year's cleaner secular trades. Instability atop Kyiv's command is not what the re-armament complex wants to see priced. Onto markets.

## SEGMENT ONE — MARKET OVERVIEW

ALEX: Thursday's close, and it was a second straight down day led by the AI complex. The S&P 500 off 51 basis points to 7,533.77, the Nasdaq down a heavier 1.5% to 25,881.95, and the Dow far more resilient — off just 20 basis points, down 105.67 points, to 52,552.97.

SAM: So a growth-and-chips problem, not a market problem. The Dow barely moved.

ALEX: Right. Under the hood the breadth wasn't nearly as ugly as the Nasdaq headline — it was concentrated in the biggest, most crowded AI names. And the proximate trigger was Alphabet, down more than 4% after Bloomberg reported it's behind schedule delivering Gemini 3.5 Pro, its flagship model.

SAM: A model delay knocks four percent off a company that size?

ALEX: When it's priced for AI leadership, yes. The whole terminal-value story for these names rests on staying at the frontier, so any slip in the roadmap reprices the multiple at the margin — and it pulled the semis and the memory names down with it, SanDisk and Western Digital among the hardest hit. That's the week in one trade: no forgiveness for any crack in the AI-growth story.

SAM: And the rates picture is the part that should still surprise people.

ALEX: This is the crux. June CPI landed Tuesday at 3.5% headline, below the 3.8% consensus, month-over-month minus four-tenths — the biggest drop since April 2020, almost entirely energy. Core 2.6%, flat. On paper, a green light to cut. And yet the 10-year sits around 4.57%, the 2-year near 4.17% — yields ticked up, not down.

SAM: Which is the counterintuitive bit. Cooler inflation, higher yields.

ALEX: Because the bond market is looking straight through a backward-looking, energy-driven print to a forward crude chart that just turned higher. The disinflation in that number is the very oil that's already reversing. So the July meeting — the FOMC's the twenty-eighth and twenty-ninth — isn't hold-versus-cut anymore. It's hold-versus-hike. Wednesday's PPI, minus three-tenths on the month, tells the same story: soft realized inflation, hard forward risk.

SAM: And the positioning underneath the tape? Because two down days in the crowded names smells like more than fundamentals.

ALEX: It is. What you're watching is a de-grossing in the most consensus book on the street. Everybody owns the same handful of AI winners, so when one cracks, the risk managers trim the whole basket at once — that's why a single-name model delay becomes a sector-wide 1.5% down day. It's mechanical, not a change of thesis. The equal-weight index held up far better than the cap-weight, which is the classic signature of a positioning unwind rather than a growth scare.

SAM: So a crowded-trade wobble, not a top.

ALEX: That's the base case — a crowded trade shaking out weak hands, not the end of the theme. But it's fragile precisely because it's so concentrated. When the top seven names are this much of the index, the market's health and a handful of AI roadmaps are the same sentence.

SAM: And the cross-asset tells?

ALEX: The interesting one is gold — around 4,000 dollars and drifting lower even with a live war, because a firmer dollar and higher real yields are outmuscling the haven bid. The dollar index up near 100 and a half. When gold can't rally on a Middle-East escalation, that tells you rates are the dominant force right now, not fear.

SAM: And the backdrop under all of it?

ALEX: Earnings, and they're the cushion. FactSet has Q2 S&P growth tracking north of 23%, revised up from about 19% at quarter start — an unusually large upward revision mid-season. So the E in the multiple is compounding even as the macro gets noisier.

SAM: So what actually breaks the standoff — the cool inflation or the hot oil?

ALEX: Oil, in the near term. If crude keeps climbing into the meeting, the July inflation print reverses this month's energy relief and the hold-to-hike debate tips hawkish for real. If the barrel fades, the disinflation trend reasserts and the cut comes back onto the table for the autumn. It genuinely is that binary, which is why the whole desk is watching the Strait of Hormuz instead of the dot plot. Net it out: constructive on earnings, cautious on rates, and every model runs through the price of a barrel. US business — and it's a tale of two guides.

## SEGMENT ONE — U.S. BUSINESS

SAM: US business. Two earnings that dropped after yesterday's close, and both cut straight to today's throughline. Start with the airline.

ALEX: United Airlines. Adjusted earnings of 1 dollar 99 a share, revenue 17.7 billion, up 16%, premium revenue up 16%, cargo up 23%, a pre-tax margin near 6%. And here's the line that matters: they raised full-year adjusted EPS guidance to 9-to-11 dollars — while absorbing nearly 6 billion dollars of extra fuel cost this year. The fuel bill alone was up 2.3 billion in the quarter, 84%, worth more than a dollar a share.

SAM: So this is the oil story with a P&L attached. A company eating the crude spike in real time and still guiding up.

ALEX: That's exactly why it's the tell. The demand side — premium cabins, corporate travel, international, cargo — is strong enough to eat an 84% jump in jet fuel and still raise the number. It tells you the consumer at the top end is intact, and it tells you where the oil shock actually lands first: on margins, quietly, not on demand. If crude keeps climbing, that 6-billion becomes the headline instead of the footnote, and the guide gets a lot harder to defend.

SAM: Second name — UnitedHealth, and it was a monster.

ALEX: A blowout. Adjusted earnings of 6 dollars 38 against about 4.90 expected, revenue 112 billion, and the number the whole managed-care complex watches — the medical loss ratio — improved to 86.7%. They raised full-year adjusted EPS to 19.50-to-20 dollars, and the stock jumped around 7%.

SAM: After the year that sector's had, a clean beat on the loss ratio is a real signal.

ALEX: It's the signal. The bear case on managed care all year was that medical costs were running away from them — utilization normalizing higher, seniors using more care. A loss ratio back under 87% with raised guidance says they've got their arms around it again. And notice the rotation it captures: in a tape punishing every growth name that decelerates, here's a defensive healthcare giant re-rating higher on execution. Money leaving the crowded AI trade has to go somewhere, and yesterday it went into the boring compounder that beat.

SAM: And the read-through for the rest of the group?

ALEX: That's why it's a market event, not just a company event — UnitedHealth is the bellwether, so a loss-ratio beat de-risks the read into the rest of the managed-care names reporting over the next two weeks. If the biggest, most complex book in the industry got costs under control, the smaller players probably did too. It's the kind of print that quietly resets an entire sector's multiple.

SAM: Two guides, two directions — the whole market in a pair of earnings. International next.

## SEGMENT ONE — INTERNATIONAL BUSINESS & POLITICS

ALEX: Two across the Atlantic. First, pharma M&A: Novartis is buying the UK biotech Myricx Bio for up to 1.5 billion dollars — 1.1 billion upfront, up to 400 million in milestones. It's a next-generation antibody-drug-conjugate platform, a novel payload chemistry, with lead oncology assets aimed at B7-H3 and HER2. Closing expected in the second half.

SAM: Another big-pharma bolt-on into the ADC race.

ALEX: And that's the pattern to hold. Antibody-drug conjugates are the hottest modality in oncology — a targeted warhead that delivers chemo straight to the tumor — and with the patent cliffs looming late-decade, the majors are buying the next platform rather than waiting to invent it. Small, targeted, science-led deals. When organic growth is scarce, the balance sheets go shopping, and biotech is where they shop.

SAM: Second, a genuine political milestone in Britain — Labour has its new leader.

ALEX: Andy Burnham is set to be elected Labour leader today, and to replace Starmer as prime minister on Monday. We flagged this as a slow-burn gilt story when Starmer stepped down; now it's real. Burnham runs to Starmer's left, so the market question is fiscal — does he reopen the spending envelope Rachel Reeves spent two years defending?

SAM: And the gilt market's answer so far?

ALEX: No gap move yet — this isn't a Truss-style event, there's no fiscal statement attached to the handover. But this is exactly the kind of thing that sits under the long end for months. If the market starts to sniff a looser fiscal stance under a Burnham government, it re-rates the 30-year first, and it shows up in sterling. Watch the gilt curve into his first budget, whenever that lands. A change of prime minister on Monday is a slow-burn risk, not a same-day one. China to close the block.

## SEGMENT ONE — CHINA

ALEX: The fresh China story isn't a data print — it's leverage. The IEA's new Global Critical Minerals Outlook, out yesterday, warns that if China fully implements its expanded rare-earth export controls, it could put roughly 6.5 trillion dollars of downstream Western production at risk — autos, high-tech, defense, and clean energy.

SAM: 6.5 trillion. Put that number in context, because it's enormous.

ALEX: It's the estimated value of all the manufacturing that depends on those inputs — the magnets and motors inside an EV, a wind turbine, a fighter jet, a data-center cooling system. Beijing broadened the controls last October and then delayed full implementation by a year, and that delay is the tell: it's a loaded weapon sitting on the table through the trade talks. And the exposure isn't evenly shared — the US and Europe would absorb nearly half the hit.

SAM: So it's the mirror image of the AI-power story we keep telling. Instead of being short electrons, the West is short the minerals.

ALEX: That's the sharp read, and it's why this is more than a headline. The entire re-industrialization push — reshoring, defense rebuild, the energy transition — runs on a supply chain China dominates end to end, from the mine to the refined magnet. This report is just the IEA putting a number on the chokehold. It reframes every reshoring announcement: you can permit the factory, pour the concrete, and still not have the neodymium to run it. Strategic dependency with a price tag attached.

SAM: And we've seen the dress rehearsal for how China uses this.

ALEX: We have — the gallium and germanium curbs a couple of years back were the proof of concept, targeted controls that sent Western buyers scrambling and prices spiking. Rare earths are that same playbook at a hundred times the scale, because there's no quick substitute and no Western refining capacity that can spin up in under a decade. The investable read is that anything tied to ex-China supply — a Western magnet maker, a rare-earth miner, a recycler — carries a strategic premium now that has nothing to do with this quarter's earnings and everything to do with who controls the inputs.

SAM: That's the block. Changing gears to the desk this whole show keeps circling.

## SEGMENT TWO — ENERGY / DATA CENTERS / UTILITIES

SAM: Segment two — the energy desk. And Alex, the fresh angle today isn't the queue or the auction we keep tracking. It's the bill.

ALEX: It's the bill, and it just went from theory to a number on a statement. PPL in Pennsylvania raised residential rates 4.9% on July first — about 7 dollars and 42 cents a month on a thousand-kilowatt-hour bill — and, crucially, it created a new large-load "data-center" rate class. That's the first Pennsylvania utility to try to wall off data-center costs from ordinary ratepayers.

SAM: Which is the fight we said would turn political the moment it hit people's wallets.

ALEX: And now it has. For contrast, take Virginia — the data-center capital of the world — where a resident's January bill hit 281 dollars, roughly three times normal. So you've got two models crystallizing in real time. Model one, the Virginia path: the grid upgrades for hyperscaler load get socialized into everyone's bill. Model two, the new Pennsylvania path: you create a separate rate class and make the data centers pay for the capacity they cause. Every state regulator in the country is about to have to choose between those two, and it's a genuinely hard political question.

SAM: And Washington is leaning in.

ALEX: The White House is convening a second, expanded ratepayer-protection summit, and Michigan's governor rolled out a data-center energy and water plan this week. This is the moment the AI buildout stops being a pure capex story and becomes a cost-of-living story. Power is turning into the political variable that gasoline prices always were — and in an election-conscious environment, no one wants to be the governor who let grandma's bill triple to subsidize a hyperscaler.

SAM: Meanwhile the physical build doesn't wait for the regulators. What's new on the supply side?

ALEX: The equipment tells the real timeline better than any forecast. GE Vernova's gas-turbine backlog is now up to 100 gigawatts, from 83 in the first quarter, and they're targeting north of 110 by year-end. And last week Baker Hughes and Kodiak signed a behind-the-meter turbine deal — 1 gigawatt by 2030, scalable to 1.8 — purpose-built to power data centers on-site.

SAM: Behind-the-meter because the grid queue is too slow.

ALEX: That's the whole logic. When you can't wait years for an interconnection, you build your own generation on the fence line and skip the grid entirely. It's faster, but it's also a quiet disintermediation of the utility — the biggest new loads in the country generating their own power and barely touching the public system. And those turbine order books, sold out into the back half of the decade, are the clearest proof anywhere that this buildout is real, whatever the stocks did this week.

SAM: There's a nasty feedback loop hiding in that, though.

ALEX: There is, and it's the one regulators are starting to fear. If the largest, most creditworthy loads self-supply and leave the grid, the fixed cost of the whole system — the poles, the wires, the peaker plants — gets spread across a smaller base of ordinary ratepayers. That's the utility death-spiral in miniature: the hyperscalers get cheap, fast, dedicated power, and the household left on the public system pays more to hold up infrastructure the data center no longer leans on. Which is exactly why that Pennsylvania rate class matters so much — it's an early attempt to keep the big loads paying their share.

SAM: Which connects right back to the trade.

ALEX: It does — that's the gap the whole market is wrestling with. The IEA has global data-center electricity demand doubling by 2030, and the AI slice tripling. The physical evidence — turbines, transformers, nuclear PPAs, on-site gas — says the build is accelerating. And yet the equities can sell off 4% on a model delay. Fundamentals roaring, sentiment wobbling. Chips get the headlines; electrons and turbines are the binding constraint.

SAM: And the through-line to inflation.

ALEX: It's direct, and it's the uncomfortable part. Power bills going up, fuel bills going up, critical minerals at strategic risk — the AI build and the oil shock are pushing on the exact same cost structure the Fed is trying to bring to heel. That's why the disinflation in Tuesday's CPI feels so fragile. The cheap-energy tailwind of the spring has become a headwind. Bring it home to the Philippines.

## SEGMENT THREE — PHILIPPINES

SAM: Segment three — the Philippines, and this is where the oil bill bites hardest. Weather first.

ALEX: Quieter skies than last week — no typhoon on the landmass right now. PAGASA is tracking a low-pressure area well east of Mindanao with a high chance of becoming a tropical depression, but it's not expected to hit land for two to three days. The active story is the habagat, the southwest monsoon, soaking the far north of Luzon today with flash-flood and landslide warnings across Ilocos and the Cordilleras.

SAM: Now the macro, and the new data point landed yesterday.

ALEX: It did — remittances, and they printed soft. Cash remittances up just 2% in May, to 2.71 billion dollars, the slowest monthly pace in years, even though the first-half total is still a record 14.11 billion. That 2% is the number to sit with.

SAM: Because remittances are the shock absorber.

ALEX: Exactly — they're roughly a tenth of GDP, the most reliable dollar inflow the country has, and the thing that keeps household consumption steady when the peso wobbles. So watching that growth rate flatten toward 2% tells you the cushion is thinning at precisely the wrong moment — because the shock on the other side is getting bigger.

SAM: The shock being the oil bill.

ALEX: Right. The Philippines imports something like 98% of its oil, so every dollar Hormuz adds to crude widens the import tab, pressures a peso already pinned near its record low around 61.7 to the dollar, and feeds the inflation the central bank is already fighting at a 4.75% policy rate. The June relief we all cheered a month ago is precisely what's reversing on them now. So the shock absorber is thinning right as the shock gets heavier — that's the bind in one sentence.

SAM: And the central bank's room to maneuver?

ALEX: Narrow, and getting narrower. They're already at 4.75% after two straight hikes, and the peso near a record low means every cut risks importing more inflation through a weaker currency. So the BSP is leaning on reserves to smooth the peso rather than spend its rate ammunition — you can see the intervention capping the currency just under 61.7. It's a holding action: defend the peso, keep policy tight, and pray the oil bid fades before the reserves or the political patience runs out.

SAM: Structurally intact, cyclically squeezed.

ALEX: That's the honest summary. The long-term story is still there — a young workforce, and the two great dollar earners, remittances and the outsourcing sector. But cyclically it's an economy absorbing an external oil shock with a weakening currency and a thinning buffer, hostage to a crude chart being set an ocean away. A little local color to close: the president declared eight local holidays across July — this week the Araw ng Mauban in Quezon, and later the Kinabayo Festival in Dapitan. Life goes on around the macro, as it always does.

SAM: Onward — and this next one is my favorite.

## SEGMENT FOUR — VOCAB OF THE DAY

SAM: Segment four — vocab of the day. Two Mandarin, two Tagalog, all tied to today's stories, and we do this one in-language. Alex, start us in Mandarin.

ALEX: 第一个词是"尽管",意思是"虽然、即使如此"。造个句子:尽管芯片股昨天大跌,AI 的建设还在加速。(The first word is "jǐnguǎn," "although / even so." A sentence: although chip stocks plunged yesterday, the AI buildout keeps accelerating.)

SAM: 尽管……还是……——所以它常常跟"还是"或"仍然"搭配?(So "jǐnguǎn… háishì…" — it often pairs with "háishì" or "réngrán," "still"?)

ALEX: 对,这是固定搭配,语气比较书面、比较强。(Right, that's the fixed pairing, and the tone is more formal and emphatic.) 跟"虽然"比,"尽管"更强调"不管阻力多大,结果照样发生"。(Compared with "suīrán," "although," "jǐnguǎn" stresses that no matter the obstacle, the outcome happens anyway.) 尽管市场恐慌,基本面还是很强。(Despite the market panic, the fundamentals are still strong.)

SAM: 我懂了——"尽管"带一点"顶着压力"的意思。第二个词呢?(I get it — "jǐnguǎn" carries a sense of pushing against pressure. And the second word?)

ALEX: 第二个词是"加剧",意思是"变得更严重"。(The second word is "jiājù," to intensify or aggravate — for something to get more severe.) 中东局势加剧,通胀压力也随之加剧。(As the Middle East situation intensifies, inflationary pressure intensifies right along with it.)

SAM: "加剧"和"增加"有什么区别?(What's the difference between "jiājù" and "zēngjiā"?)

ALEX: "增加"只是数量变多,是中性的;"加剧"是负面的、紧张的东西变得更强烈。(Zēngjiā is just a neutral increase in quantity; jiājù is when something tense or negative grows more intense.) 这是书面语,财经新闻里到处都是:紧张局势加剧,通胀压力加剧,矛盾加剧。(It's formal, written register — all over financial news: tensions intensify, inflation pressure intensifies, contradictions sharpen.)

SAM: 太好了。现在换菲律宾语。(Excellent. Now let's switch to Tagalog.)

ALEX: Ang unang salita ay "igting" — ang tindi o tensyon ng isang sitwasyong hila nang hila. (The first word is "igting" — the intensity or tension of a situation stretched tighter and tighter.) Lalong tumindi ang igting sa Gitnang Silangan ngayong linggo. (The tension in the Middle East intensified further this week.)

SAM: Ano ang pagkakaiba ng "igting" sa "init"? (What's the difference between "igting" and "init," heat?)

ALEX: Ang "init" ay galit o tensyong pulitikal na pansamantala; ang "igting" ay ang paghigpit ng sitwasyon, parang lubid na binabatak. (Init is anger or passing political heat; igting is the tightening of a situation, like a rope pulled taut.) Kaya bagay na bagay sa merkado at sa digmaan: igting ng benta, igting ng labanan. (So it fits markets and war alike: the intensity of a selloff, the intensity of the fighting.) Ito ang katumbas ng Mandarin na 加剧. (It's the counterpart of the Mandarin jiājù.)

SAM: Magandang tulay 'yan. At ang huling salita? (Nice bridge. And the last word?)

ALEX: "Pasanin" — ang bigat na dinadala mo sa balikat mo. Mabigat na pasanin ng bawat OFW ang kanyang pamilya, lalo na ngayong mahina ang piso. (The burden you carry on your shoulders. Each overseas worker carries the heavy burden of family, especially now with a weak peso.)

SAM: Iba ito sa "dalahin," tama? (This is different from "dalahin," right?)

ALEX: Tama. Ang "dalahin" ay simpleng bagay na binubuhat; ang "pasanin" ay mabigat, madalas hindi pisikal — responsibilidad, hirap. (Correct. "Dalahin" is a simple object you carry; a "pasanin" is heavy, often not physical — responsibility, hardship.) At may kaugnay: ang "pasan-pasan," ibig sabihin, buong-buong dinadala mo. (And there's a relative — "pasan-pasan," meaning you shoulder the whole of it.) Isang salita, buong ekonomiya ng pamilya. (One word, and it holds a whole family economy.)

SAM: Language living inside the news. Two Mandarin, two Tagalog — a holding-firm-despite, an intensifying, a tension pulled taut, and a burden carried. That's today's show in four words.

ALEX: Couldn't have scripted the throughline better.
