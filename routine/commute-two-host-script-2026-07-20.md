# The Morning Commute — Two-Host Script (LONG / 1.5x edition)
### Monday, July 20, 2026

**Voices:** ALEX = Host A, domain expert (Sulafat). SAM = Host B, sharp generalist (Charon).
**Style (render):** Two polished financial-news anchors — crisp, authoritative, controlled energy, British accent.
**Audience assumption:** fluent in markets and current events. No 101 explainers; lead with the number and the second-order read.
**Segment rules:** open straight into the first story — NO run-of-show / segment preview in the cold open. Close with a ~1 min "One Good Thing" and a warm sign-off.

> RENDER NOTE: Only `ALEX:` / `SAM:` lines are spoken. `##` headers are silent guides. Hosts announce each segment aloud. ONE line per turn. Numbers as NUMERALS.

## COLD OPEN

SAM: Good morning, this is The Morning Commute. Monday, July twentieth. I'm Sam.

ALEX: And I'm Alex. Here's the throughline for the week's open: the AI-stock wobble stopped being about one name and became about the whole complex. Friday was the third straight down day, and this time it broadened — the Dow, which had been the hiding place all week, fell too, and the selling spread clear across the Pacific into a China-tech rout. The trigger moved on, from a single model delay to a structural cost scare, with a Netflix growth crack on top. And underneath it, the same vise keeps tightening — oil bid on fresh Iran risk, yields stuck high, gold near 4,000 dollars, a live hold-versus-hike Fed just eight days out. Yet the physical build behind the whole trade only accelerates. Fundamentals roaring, prices flinching. Let's get into it.

## SEGMENT ONE — HEADLINES

SAM: Headlines. Two from the world desk before we hit the tape, and the first is a genuine landmark out of Sudan.

ALEX: An army-aligned court has sentenced the Rapid Support Forces chief, Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo — the man everyone knows as Hemedti — to death, in absentia, for genocide and crimes against humanity in Darfur. Roughly a dozen to fifteen senior RSF figures were sentenced alongside him, including his own brothers, tied to the 2023 atrocities in el-Geneina and the killing of the West Darfur governor. And Britain layered fresh sanctions on top, targeting RSF commanders and the gold-and-finance networks bankrolling the war.

SAM: A death sentence for a warlord who is nowhere near a courtroom. What does a verdict with no arrest power actually change?

ALEX: On the ground, not much tomorrow — there's no one to serve the warrant. But it hardens the split. It formally brands one side of this civil war a genocidal enterprise, which slams the door on the negotiated settlement diplomats keep reaching for, and it puts a legal target on the money. And the market-adjacent read is the gold. Sudan's war is financed through smuggled gold moving out through the Gulf, so tighter scrutiny of those chains keeps a risk premium on the whole Red Sea and Sahel corridor — the same corridor a lot of the world's shipping and a chunk of its informal gold trade run through.

SAM: So a symbolic verdict with a very real financial tail. Second world story — and it's a fight inside the European Union itself.

ALEX: It is. Denmark, which took over the rotating EU Council presidency on the first of July, is formally pushing to trigger Article 7 — the so-called nuclear option — to strip Hungary of its voting rights in the bloc. The Danish minister Marie Bjerre is leading it, and the grievance is Viktor Orban's serial vetoes on Ukraine's accession, plus what Brussels calls core-values breaches on rule of law.

SAM: Article 7 is the thing everyone always threatens and never does. Is this actually different?

ALEX: Procedurally it's still uphill — you need near-unanimity for the harshest step, and France and Germany haven't fully signed on, so an actual suspension isn't happening next week. But the fact that the presidency is driving it out in the open is the signal. This is really a proxy war over Ukraine. Orban has been the single point of failure blocking accession talks and tranches of aid, and if the bloc ever found a way to route around Budapest's veto, it re-rates everything Ukraine-exposed — the reconstruction names, the aid timeline, and the political-risk premium sitting on the euro. Watch it as a slow burn, not a same-day move. To the tape.

## SEGMENT ONE — MARKET OVERVIEW

ALEX: Friday's close, and it was ugly and — the important word — broad. The S&P 500 fell 1.01%, off 76 points to 7,457.69. The Nasdaq took the brunt again, down 1.4%, off 362 points to 25,520.24. But the tell was the Dow: down 0.77%, more than 400 points, to 52,146.42. On Thursday the Dow had barely moved while the Nasdaq bled — a clean growth-only story. Friday, the hiding place stopped hiding.

SAM: So this is the part that matters. Two days ago it was a chips-and-growth problem. Friday it was a market problem.

ALEX: Exactly — the selling spread. That's the whole character change. When it's just the crowded AI names, you can call it a positioning unwind. When the Dow gives back 400 points on the same day, the de-grossing has widened into something closer to a broad risk-off. And the proximate trigger evolved with it. Thursday it was Alphabet and a model delay. Friday it was structural: TSMC guided capital spending higher than the street wanted, which spooked everyone about the cost curve of the whole AI build, and — the sharper one — a Chinese lab, Moonshot, dropped a model they're calling Kimi K3, billed as the largest openly downloadable open-weight model yet.

SAM: And an open-weight model that good is a dagger at the "more compute forever" thesis.

ALEX: That's precisely why the semis took it hardest. If a free, downloadable model closes the gap on the frontier, the market starts asking whether the trillion-dollar compute build is as defensible as the multiples assume. AMD was down 5% intraday, Intel off 4%, Nvidia down 3% before trimming to close off 2.2% at 202.81. The whole megacap block went with it — Meta down 2.8%, Tesla off 2.6%, Alphabet down 2.2%. The philadelphia semiconductor index is now off 13% in a month.

SAM: And the single-name gut-punch was Netflix.

ALEX: It was — down 7.3% Friday to a fifty-two-week low, and it traded down as much as 12% at the worst. The print itself was fine: earnings 80 cents, revenue 12.56 billion, up 13%. What did the damage was the guide — third-quarter revenue guided to 12.86 billion, a hair light of the 13 billion the street wanted — plus a decision to stop reporting engagement data twice a year and only disclose it annually.

SAM: So they dimmed the lights on their own metrics in the exact week the market has zero tolerance for it.

ALEX: Zero tolerance is right. In this tape, any growth name that decelerates and then reduces disclosure gets taken to the woodshed. It's the same reflex that hit Alphabet — the market is repricing every richly-valued growth story at the first hint the trajectory is flattening. Netflix is now on track for its worst year since 2022.

SAM: And underneath the equity drama, the rates and commodity picture is the part that should still unsettle people.

ALEX: This is the crux, and it hasn't changed — it's gotten more acute. The 10-year sits at 4.55%, the 2-year at 4.18%, the 30-year at 5.06%. Yields are not coming down to rescue equities, and the reason is oil. West Texas crude jumped 4.5% on Friday to about 82 and a half dollars, Brent up near 88, on fresh US-Iran violence and the same Hormuz shipping risk we keep circling. So the bond market is looking straight through Wednesday's cool June inflation print — 3.5% headline, 2.6% core — to a forward oil chart that just turned higher again.

SAM: And gold, with a live war and a shaky stock market, is doing what?

ALEX: Sitting around 4,030 dollars and going nowhere — which is itself the message. When gold can't catch a bid with equities falling and missiles flying, it's telling you high real yields and a firm dollar are the dominant force, not fear. The dollar's strong, the yen is pinned at a shockingly weak 162.4 to the dollar, near its cycle low. Rates are running this market.

SAM: So what does all of that do to the Fed, eight days out?

ALEX: It boxes them further in. The July meeting is the twenty-eighth and twenty-ninth, no new dot plot attached. And the debate is not hold-versus-cut anymore — it's hold-versus-hike. Markets have a hold as the overwhelming base case, but the only alternative anyone is pricing is a hike, roughly a one-in-five tail, because core is stuck at 2.6% and the oil bid threatens to reverse this month's energy relief. A cut is simply not in the conversation. Constructive on earnings, cautious on rates, and every model still runs through the price of a barrel. US business.

ALEX: Fifth Third. Second-quarter net income 763 million dollars, GAAP earnings 83 cents — that actually missed the street's 84 by a penny — but adjusted earnings were 1.02. The number that matters is net interest income: up 48% year on year to 2.22 billion. Because this was the first full quarter with Comerica folded in — the 10.9 billion-dollar acquisition that closed earlier this year and made Fifth Third the ninth-largest bank in the country.

SAM: So the marquee regional-bank merger of the cycle is finally showing up in the financials.

ALEX: And that's the story — the deal thesis was always scale and a bigger interest-margin engine, and you can see the engine in that 48% NII jump. The penny miss on the headline is the flip side: integration costs and one-time charges still weigh while you're bolting two banks together. But this is the number regional-bank investors wanted — proof the consolidation wave is translating into earnings power, not just press releases. In a year when the mid-cap banks needed a reason to re-rate, a working merger is it.

SAM: Second name — and it was the rare clean beat on a filthy day.

ALEX: Travelers, and it was a blowout. Core income of 10.04 a share against a street around 5.33 — call it an 88% beat. Net income 2.2 billion, revenue 12.15 billion. And the number the whole insurance complex watches, the combined ratio, came in at 83.6% — anything under 100 is an underwriting profit, and the low 80s is exceptional.

SAM: What drove a beat that size — is it real, or is it one-off?

ALEX: A bit of both, honestly. Catastrophe losses ran about half what they did a year ago — a quieter quarter for storms — and they booked a 578 million-dollar favorable reserve release, which means prior years' claims came in cheaper than they'd set aside. That's not repeatable every quarter. But the underlying underwriting margin is genuinely strong, and here's why it fits the day: on a tape punishing every growth name that so much as blinks, a boring property-casualty insurer printed a huge beat and was one of the only large caps green on Friday, next to Exxon and Apple. Money leaving the crowded AI trade has to land somewhere, and Friday it went to the compounder that beat.

SAM: Two financials, both telling you where the safety is right now. International.

## SEGMENT ONE — INTERNATIONAL BUSINESS & POLITICS

ALEX: Two from abroad, and the first keeps building one of the year's cleanest secular trades. Saab — the Swedish defense group — beat on the second quarter Friday, and the headline is the backlog: a record order book, powered by a Polish submarine order that added something like 47 billion Swedish kronor, roughly 7 billion dollars, of new bookings in the quarter alone. The chief executive used the results to lecture Europe about overhauling how it buys weapons.

SAM: So the European rearmament story isn't slowing — it's compounding.

ALEX: It's compounding, and that's the point to hold. We've flagged the continental defense build as a structural, multi-year capex cycle, and a record backlog at Saab is the hard evidence — order books that stretch years out, not a single good quarter. It's why the defense primes — Saab, Rheinmetall, BAE Systems — keep re-rating even on days the broad market is falling apart. When the tape sells off but the backlog names hold, that's the market telling you which themes it believes are real.

SAM: Second one — Airbus, and it's really a China-and-geopolitics story wearing an aviation jacket.

ALEX: Nicely put. Airbus landed a fresh round of orders from Chinese state carriers on Friday — Air China taking 15 A350-900 widebodies, Shenzhen Airlines 40 of the A320neo family. And the subtext is everything: China is steering its aircraft demand to Europe while Boeing is mired in its own troubles.

SAM: So Beijing is using plane orders as a diplomatic instrument.

ALEX: Exactly — aircraft orders are one of the most visible geopolitical bargaining chips there is, because each one is billions of dollars and thousands of jobs, and Beijing knows precisely which capitals feel it. Tilting that demand toward Toulouse and away from an American manufacturer is a message wrapped in a purchase order. For Airbus it's a real win — it extends their dominance in the single most important growth market in aviation, and it deepens their backlog at exactly the moment Boeing can least afford to lose ground. China to close the block.

## SEGMENT ONE — CHINA

ALEX: And China is where Friday's selloff went to get worse. While Wall Street was falling, the Chinese tech complex was in a genuine rout: the Shanghai Composite down 3.1%, the STAR 50 — Shanghai's answer to the Nasdaq — off a brutal 7.1%, and Hong Kong's tech index down more than 4%. This wasn't a spillover from New York. It was its own bubble getting a leg cut out.

SAM: And the backdrop is almost surreal, because Beijing was simultaneously throwing itself a party for AI.

ALEX: That's the contradiction of the whole thing. All this happened around the World Artificial Intelligence Conference in Shanghai, where Xi Jinping gave a speech pushing global AI cooperation, and China and 28 other nations signed on to a new Shanghai-based body — they're calling it the World AI Cooperation Organization, an explicit bid to write the world's AI-governance rules from Shanghai rather than Washington. And under that official optimism, the froth was extreme: Zhipu's latest GLM model, a startup called MiniMax teasing a 2.7-trillion-parameter monster, and Moonshot's Kimi K3 — the same open-weight model that helped knock the American semis down.

SAM: So the same model that spooked Nvidia spooked its own home market harder.

ALEX: Which is the sharp read. The Chinese AI names had run on pure euphoria — governance leadership, model-size bragging rights, national-champion status — and when the market started to ask whether any of it makes money, the de-rating was violent, far worse than the US version. And it lands right on top of the two-speed economy we keep describing: a technology sector priced for a revolution sitting on top of a real economy with soft consumption and stubborn deflationary pressure. Beijing wants to lead the world on AI rules while its own AI stocks just told you what happens when narrative meets valuation. That's the block. To the desk this whole show keeps circling back to.

## SEGMENT TWO — ENERGY / DATA CENTERS / UTILITIES

SAM: Segment two — the energy desk. And Alex, here's the thing that makes today's data so striking: while the AI stocks were being cut apart, the physical evidence that the build is accelerating got more concrete, not less. Start with the capacity auction.

ALEX: Start with PJM, because the number is almost poetic in how broken it is. PJM — the biggest grid operator in America, serving 65 million people — ran its 2028-2029 capacity auction, and the results landed Monday the fourteenth. It cleared roughly 138,000 megawatts, and it cleared at 325 dollars per megawatt-day — which is the legislated price cap. The absolute ceiling. Third auction in a row pinned to its own cap.

SAM: And clearing at the cap sounds bullish for generators, but you're telling me it's actually a broken signal.

ALEX: It's a broken signal, and that's the whole insight. Look underneath: the auction cleared about 6.8 gigawatts below the reliability requirement — it literally could not buy enough capacity to meet the target — and it drew only about 500 megawatts of genuinely new supply. FERC's new chair, Laura Swett, said that out loud on the sixteenth. So the market is screaming scarcity at maximum price, and the response is 500 megawatts of new build. A functioning market clears higher and summons supply. This one has hit its ceiling and summoned almost nothing.

SAM: So the price mechanism has stopped doing its job.

ALEX: It's stopped doing its job, and that's the story of the whole sector right now. When the capacity market can't call forth new plants even at its legal maximum, scarcity stops being a price-discovery problem and becomes a governance problem — which is exactly why the action is moving to Washington. And that brings us to the single most important thing happening today, literally today, that almost nobody outside this desk is watching.

SAM: Which is?

ALEX: Today, July twentieth, is the deadline for all six of the country's grid operators — PJM, MISO, SPP, California, New England, and New York — to file generation-adequacy reports with FERC, under show-cause orders FERC issued back on June eighteenth. This is the federal government stepping in to standardize the rules for how these enormous data-center loads connect to the grid, and how co-located power — a data center bolting its own gas plant on behind the meter — gets treated across every market at once.

SAM: So instead of every state and every utility improvising, Washington is trying to write one rulebook.

ALEX: One rulebook, and the stakes are the cost-shift. FERC named five reform priorities, and the big one is protecting ordinary ratepayers from subsidizing the grid upgrades these hyperscalers trigger — plus a formal study process for generators sitting "electrically proximate" to a large load. Get this right and the behind-the-meter gas deals get a clean, repeatable tariff path. Get it wrong and every one of them stays bespoke and litigated for years. The outcome quietly decides how fast the whole build can actually plug in.

SAM: And put the scale of the demand next to the scale of the supply, because that gap is the investable fact.

ALEX: Bank of America did exactly that mid-month, and the numbers are stark. Data centers alone add about 125 gigawatts of US electricity load between now and 2030. Total power demand growing at a 4.1% compound rate — which sounds small until you remember US power demand was essentially flat for two decades. And here's the gap: BofA models more than 230 gigawatts of capacity demand against roughly 93 gigawatts of supply. Call it a 100-gigawatt shortfall by 2030.

SAM: A hundred gigawatts short. That's not a rounding error, that's a hundred large power plants that don't exist.

ALEX: And the binding constraint is not money — it's turbines. Large gas turbines are sold out through 2030. You cannot buy a slot. So developers are pivoting to on-site gas engines — reciprocating engines, the kind that can be delivered in quarters not years — while utilities quietly extend the life of coal plants, bolt on batteries, and race to build transmission. When the constraint is a manufacturing queue and not capital, the whole game becomes who has secured their megawatts, physically, on paper, today.

SAM: And you can see that in how the deals are now being structured.

ALEX: You can, and it's the sharpest change in the market. Look at the joint venture that launched on the fifteenth — Coravel, a fifty-fifty between the Spanish builder ACS and BlackRock's infrastructure arm. Their very first act was to sign a hyperscaler lease — about 140 megawatts of critical IT load at a Dallas-Fort Worth campus, with rights to another 100 on top, and a pipeline of 1.7 gigawatts behind it. And the model is the point: they wrap the power, the building, and the operations into a single contract.

SAM: Power, shell, and operations sold as one product. That's a direct threat to the plain-vanilla data-center landlord.

ALEX: It's an existential threat to the standalone developer who can only offer the building and says "go find your own electricity." When power is the scarce input, whoever controls the megawatts controls the deal — and the capital pooling into this, BlackRock-scale capital, is betting the integrated model wins. The land is no longer the asset. The deliverable megawatt is the asset.

SAM: And that scarcity is exactly what turns this into the cost-of-living fight we keep flagging.

ALEX: It is, and the concentration is the trigger. Digital Realty just locked up 1,440 acres near Kansas City — but the gating piece wasn't the land, it was a signed energy agreement with the local utility for 600 megawatts by early 2028, scaling to 2 gigawatts. And in one rural Missouri county, Montgomery County, Amazon and Google announced separate campuses — 10 billion dollars from one, 15 from the other. 25 billion dollars of hyperscale capex dropped into a single county's utility footprint.

SAM: And that whole cost lands on one small utility and its existing customers.

ALEX: That's the powder keg. When you concentrate that much new load on one utility, the grid upgrades to serve it get spread across everybody's bill — and that's precisely the ratebase fight that started in Virginia and Pennsylvania and is now going nationwide. It's why FERC's rulebook today matters so much: the entire question is whether grandmother's power bill subsidizes a hyperscaler, or the hyperscaler pays for the capacity it causes. The stocks wobbled on Friday. The build did not. Bring it home to the Philippines.

## SEGMENT THREE — PHILIPPINES

SAM: Segment three — the Philippines, where that same oil bid we keep tracking lands as a very direct squeeze. Weather first, and the good news is it's quiet.

ALEX: Quiet for once. As of Sunday and into today, PAGASA has no tropical cyclone in the country, no low-pressure area worth naming, and no wind signals up anywhere. What's active is just the habagat — the southwest monsoon — bringing isolated afternoon downpours to the western side of the country. Over the weekend the rain focused on the far north of Luzon: Ilocos, Zambales, Batanes, the Babuyan Islands, Apayao, Abra — the usual flash-flood and landslide watch during the heavy cells, but nothing widespread or prolonged. Metro Manila hit about 33 and a half degrees today.

SAM: A rare calm week on the weather map. So the story is the macro, and the macro is a squeeze.

ALEX: It is, and it starts with the central bank. The BSP is holding its policy rate at 4.75% after hiking a quarter-point in June — its second straight hike. And it turned more hawkish, not less: it raised its own 2026 inflation forecast to 6.4%, and pegged 2027 at 4.5% — both above the 2-to-4% target band. Governor Remolona called June's move "pre-emptive" and pointedly did not rule out more. The street — MUFG, Nomura, Citi — is penciling in another 50 basis points of hikes ahead.

SAM: A central bank hiking into a slowdown. That only happens when it's defending something.

ALEX: It's defending the currency and fighting imported inflation — and you can see why in the prints. Headline inflation did cool to 6.4% in June from 6.8%, which sounds like relief. But look under it: core inflation actually rose, to 4.4% from 4.1%. The cooling was all transport and food — the volatile stuff — while the underlying, stickier pressure got worse. That's the worst kind of inflation mix for a central banker: the headline improves, the core hardens.

SAM: And the peso is the pressure gauge.

ALEX: The peso is sitting at 61.67 to the dollar — a hair off the record low around 61.75 it touched earlier in the week, and down more than 4% on the year. And the mechanism is brutally simple and ties straight to our throughline: the Philippines imports almost all of its oil, so every dollar that Hormuz and the Iran risk adds to crude widens the import bill, pressures the peso, and feeds the very inflation the BSP is fighting. You can see it in the trade numbers — the May trade deficit blew out to 5.5 billion dollars, more than half again wider than a year ago, with fuel imports alone at 1.75 billion in the month.

SAM: So it's the global oil story with a national price tag attached.

ALEX: That's exactly it — a fuel-importing economy absorbing an oil shock with a currency near a record low and a central bank forced to keep tightening into it. The BSP is leaning on reserves to smooth the peso rather than spend all its rate ammunition at once. It's a holding action: defend the currency, keep policy tight, and hope the crude bid fades before patience does. And there's a big political week landing on top of it — President Marcos delivers his fifth State of the Nation Address next Monday, the twenty-seventh, and the cost of living is going to be the whole subtext, because families feel this oil-and-peso squeeze at the pump and in the market basket every single day.

SAM: Structurally intact, cyclically squeezed. Onward — and this next one is my favorite.

## SEGMENT FOUR — VOCAB OF THE DAY

SAM: Segment four — vocab of the day. Two Mandarin, two Tagalog, all tied to today's stories, and we do this one in-language. Alex, start us in Mandarin.

ALEX: 第一个词是"然而",意思是"但是、可是",不过它更正式、更书面。造个句子:AI 的建设还在加速,然而相关股票却连跌三天。(The first word is "rán'ér," meaning "however, yet" — but it's more formal, more written. A sentence: the AI buildout keeps accelerating, however the related stocks have fallen three days straight.)

SAM: 所以"然而"跟"但是"意思一样,只是语气不同?(So "rán'ér" means the same as "dànshì," just a different register?)

ALEX: 对,意思几乎一样,但"然而"用在书面语、财经评论里,"但是"更口语。(Right, nearly the same meaning, but "rán'ér" belongs to written language and financial commentary, while "dànshì" is more colloquial.) 它常常引出一个跟前面相反的、让你意外的结果。(It often introduces a result that contradicts what came before, one that surprises you.) 基本面很强,然而市场并不买账。(The fundamentals are strong; however, the market isn't buying it.)

SAM: 我懂了——用"然而"听起来更像财经社论。第二个词呢?(I see — using "rán'ér" sounds more like a financial editorial. And the second word?)

ALEX: 第二个词是"蔓延",本义是像火或藤蔓一样扩散,现在多用来形容负面情绪或危机的扩散。(The second word is "mànyán" — literally to spread like fire or a creeping vine, now mostly used for the spread of negative sentiment or a crisis.) 星期五,恐慌从科技股蔓延到整个市场,甚至蔓延到亚洲。(On Friday, the panic spread from tech stocks to the whole market, and even spread across to Asia.)

SAM: "蔓延"和"扩大"有什么不同?(What's the difference between "mànyán" and "kuòdà," to expand?)

ALEX: 好问题。"扩大"是主动地、有意地把范围做大,比如扩大投资;"蔓延"是失控地、自己往外爬,通常是坏事。(Good question. "Kuòdà" is to enlarge a scope deliberately, on purpose — like expanding investment; "mànyán" is uncontrolled, creeping outward on its own, and usually it's something bad.) 所以我们说恐慌蔓延、危机蔓延、抛售蔓延——绝不会说"恐慌扩大"那么自然。(So we say panic spreads, crisis spreads, the selloff spreads — you'd never say "panic expands" as naturally.) 这是书面语,新闻里到处都是。(It's written register, and it's everywhere in the news.)

SAM: 太贴切了,正好是今天的行情。现在换菲律宾语。(Perfectly apt — that's exactly today's market. Now let's switch to Tagalog.)

ALEX: Ang unang salita ay "lumaganap" — ang kumalat nang malawakan, parang apoy o balita na kumakalat sa lahat ng dako. (The first word is "lumaganap" — to spread widely, like a fire or news spreading everywhere.) Noong Biyernes, mabilis na lumaganap ang benta mula sa Wall Street hanggang sa Asya. (On Friday, the selloff spread fast from Wall Street all the way to Asia.)

SAM: Ano ang pagkakaiba ng "lumaganap" sa "kumalat"? (What's the difference between "lumaganap" and "kumalat"?)

ALEX: Magkalapit sila, pero ang "kumalat" ay pang-araw-araw — kumalat ang tsismis, kumalat ang sakit. Ang "lumaganap" ay mas malawak, mas pormal ang dating — paglaganap ng teknolohiya, paglaganap ng krisis. (They're close, but "kumalat" is everyday — a rumor spreads, a disease spreads. "Lumaganap" is broader and sounds more formal — the spread of technology, the spread of a crisis.) Ito ang katumbas ng Mandarin na 蔓延 na kasama natin kanina. (It's the counterpart of the Mandarin "mànyán" we just had.)

SAM: Magandang tulay 'yan. At ang huling salita? (Nice bridge. And the last word?)

ALEX: "Tiisin" — mula sa "tiis," ang matiyagang pagbata sa hirap o sakit nang hindi sumusuko. Tinitiis ng ekonomiya ng Pilipinas ang pagsipa ng langis at ang paghina ng piso. (The word is "tiisin," from "tiis" — to bear hardship or pain patiently without giving up. The Philippine economy is enduring the surge in oil and the weakening of the peso.)

SAM: Iba ba ito sa "batahin"? (Is this different from "batahin," to carry a load?)

ALEX: Oo. Ang "batahin" ay pagbubuhat ng bigat; ang "tiisin" ay pagtitiis — may tapang at pasensya, kahit masakit, nagpapatuloy ka pa rin. (Yes. "Batahin" is to lift a weight; "tiisin" is to endure — there's courage and patience in it, and even when it hurts, you keep going.) Isang salita na puno ng damdaming Pilipino: ang matiisin. (One word full of Filipino feeling — the quality of being long-suffering, "matiisin.")

SAM: Language living inside the news. Two Mandarin, two Tagalog — a formal "however," a crisis that spreads, a selloff spreading wide, and an economy enduring. That's today's show in four words.

ALEX: Couldn't have scripted the throughline better.

## SEGMENT FIVE — ARTS & CULTURE

SAM: Segment five — arts and culture. And we lead with the galleries today, not the box office. Alex, there's a major reappraisal opening in London.

ALEX: A big one, at Tate Modern — a career survey of Ana Mendieta, the Cuban-American artist, her first major UK show in over a decade. More than 120 works: the famous Silueta series, where she pressed the silhouette of her own body into earth, sand, and fire and photographed the trace; newly remastered films; and rarely seen early paintings and late sculptures. It runs into January.

SAM: Mendieta is a name a lot of people know for the tragedy of how she died, more than for the work itself.

ALEX: And that's exactly what this show sets out to correct. For years her death — the fall from a New York window in 1985, and the acquittal of her husband, the sculptor Carl Andre — has overshadowed the art. What Tate is doing is restoring the films and the objects behind those iconic photographs, and arguing that she was central, not marginal, to how we now think about the body, gender, and land in art. It's a reframing of the canon, not just a retrospective — putting a woman who worked with earth and absence back at the center of the story.

SAM: And there's a quieter rediscovery on at the National Gallery too.

ALEX: There is, and it's a lovely counterpoint — Waldmüller: Landscapes. Ferdinand Georg Waldmüller was an Austrian Biedermeier painter, early 1800s, and this is the first-ever UK exhibition devoted to him, done with the Belvedere in Vienna. The argument the show makes is that he was painting hard, clear, sunstruck Alpine light out of doors decades before the French Impressionists got the credit for it. A realist ahead of his moment, finally getting his English-language hearing.

SAM: From canvas to the concert hall — the summer festivals are in full swing.

ALEX: They are, and the standout was the First Night of the BBC Proms on Friday, at the Royal Albert Hall. Dalia Stasevska conducted the BBC Symphony in an all-American program for the United States' 250th year — Copland's Fanfare rolling into Gershwin's An American in Paris. But the moment everyone wrote about was the young pianist Yunchan Lim playing Ravel's Piano Concerto in G. The critics used words like "startling assurance" — the way he balanced the percussive bite of the outer movements against the aching lyricism of that slow middle movement.

SAM: Lim is the one who won the Van Cliburn a few years back at eighteen, isn't he — the phenomenon.

ALEX: The very same, and this is him proving the phenomenon has real depth. And there's a wall of opera behind it if you want it — Salzburg opened the same night with a new Carmen, Asmik Grigorian in the title role under Teodor Currentzis, and Bayreuth's 150th-anniversary season opens later this week. A rich few days for anyone who follows the voice. And one for the readers to close: Sigrid Nunez — the National Book Award winner behind The Friend — has just published her first-ever story collection, called It Will Come Back to You. Thirteen stories gathered across her whole career.

SAM: And the reviews on that?

ALEX: Warm — critics are saying her trademark wit and quiet empathy are actually sharper in miniature than at novel length, plot turns so gentle they barely register as turns, and never a slide into sentiment. And the timing's neat, because the book world is holding its breath for next Tuesday, the twenty-eighth, when the 2026 Booker Prize longlist drops — a panel chaired, wonderfully, by the classicist Mary Beard. So a good week to have a novelist's short stories on the nightstand while the big list gets argued over. One good thing to finish.

## SEGMENT SIX — ONE GOOD THING

SAM: And to close — one good thing, and it's a slow-motion miracle hiding in plain sight.

ALEX: It's the forests of New England. A scientist at the Woodwell Climate Research Center, Bill Moomaw, has been pointing at a fact most people who live there never notice: Massachusetts, which was roughly 90% cleared of its forest by the mid-1800s — stripped for farms and timber — is now back to about 60% forest cover. New England as a whole was logged down by around 80%, and it has quietly regrown. Moomaw calls it, flatly, "the greatest forest recovery in the history of the world."

SAM: And nobody planted it — this is the land just taking itself back.

ALEX: That's the wonderful part. When the farms moved west and the fields were abandoned, the forest simply walked back in on its own, tree by tree, over a century and a half — no grand restoration program, no ribbon-cutting. And now those regrown woods are pulling carbon out of the air, holding soil, cooling towns, and sheltering wildlife that had nowhere to go. It's a reminder that nature's default setting, given half a chance and a little time, is to heal.

SAM: A century and a half of quiet regrowth. A good one to start the week on.
