## SEGMENT ONE — CHINA
ALEX: And China is the clearest case of a central bank easing with the brakes half on. The People's Bank just announced its biggest liquidity injection in five months — about 2.1 trillion yuan of overnight reverse repos staggered across the month-end, plus a 500 billion yuan one-year policy loan, for a net add of around 100 billion.
SAM: That's a lot of plumbing. Why now?
ALEX: Because Q2 GDP cooled to 4.3%, the weakest in about three and a half years, and the month-end is always a liquidity crunch. But here's the tell: the PBOC is easing the quantity of money while holding the loan prime rate flat. It's flooding liquidity but refusing to cut the actual policy rate.
SAM: And that refusal is the oil story again.
ALEX: It is. China imports enormous volumes of crude, so a run through 100-dollar Brent narrows the room to cut rates without stoking import inflation and pressuring the yuan, which sat around 6.774. So Beijing does the thing it can — pump liquidity — and withholds the thing it can't, a rate cut. It's a debt-deflation economy that needs lower rates and an oil shock that won't let it have them. Two-speed, and boxed.
SAM: And there's a signal in preferring quantity over price that's easy to miss.
ALEX: There is. Injecting liquidity through reverse repos is temporary and reversible — it eases the month-end crunch without committing to a lower rate structure. Cutting the loan prime rate is a durable signal that lowers financing costs across the whole economy and, critically, widens the rate gap with the US, which pushes capital out and the yuan down. So the PBOC is threading it: relieve the plumbing, protect the currency. It's the policy posture of a central bank that has decided the exchange rate is the constraint that binds first.
SAM: One barrel, five policies, and China's the tightest box of all. Let's talk power.

## SEGMENT TWO — ENERGY / DATA CENTERS / UTILITIES
ALEX: This is where verdict two lives — the grid's verdict. And the single best data point this week came out of GE Vernova's quarter, because it quietly tells you the "turbines are sold out to 2030" story is only half true.
SAM: Give me the number.
ALEX: The gas turbine backlog jumped from 100 to 116 gigawatts quarter over quarter. Sounds like the wall got taller. But look at the composition: only 53 gigawatts of that is firm equipment orders. 63 gigawatts — the majority — is slot-reservation agreements. Placeholders.
SAM: So more than half the "sold out" backlog is options, not steel.
ALEX: Correct. And a slot reservation is cheap and cancelable — developers are hoarding turbine slots the exact same way they hoard interconnection-queue positions. The number that actually matters is the conversion rate: this quarter GE Vernova converted 10 gigawatts of prior reservations into firm orders, and shipped 3. That conversion pace — reservations turning into committed capacity — is the real tell, not the headline backlog.
SAM: And the second-order read?
ALEX: The turbine constraint is softer than the 116 number implies. Reservation-holders who can't reach a final investment decision eventually free those slots for developers who can. So watch cancellation and conversion disclosures the way you'd watch queue-withdrawal rates. Revenue was 11.1 billion, up 22%, and they raised the full-year guide — the business is real. But the scarcity is being overstated by a queue of optionality.
SAM: You've also got a genuinely new policy lever this week — on the transmission side.
ALEX: This is the one to file. At a Senate energy hearing Tuesday, FERC Chair Swett said the commission is standing up a grid-enhancing-technology task force — GETs. That's dynamic line ratings, advanced power-flow controllers, and high-performance conductors — reconductoring existing lines with carbon-fiber-core wire that carries roughly twice the power on the same poles and the same right-of-way.
SAM: Why does that matter against everything else we track?
ALEX: Because every constraint we usually talk about — the interconnection queue, cost allocation, 160-week transformer lead times — is on the generation or interconnection side. GETs attack the deliverability wall from the corridor side. You squeeze more megawatts through right-of-way you already own, without a new transformer or a new line. It's the one lever that can add effective transfer capacity on a 12-to-24-month clock instead of a 5-to-7-year one.
SAM: And if FERC attaches a return incentive to it?
ALEX: Then reconductoring becomes a rate-based growth line for the transmission owners — a distinct investable theme from the data-center cost-allocation fight. Watch for return-on-equity adders. And the third piece ties the turbines and the policy together: the behind-the-meter gas boom just added an oil-major counterparty. Chevron's Project Kilby, near Pecos, Texas — 2.67 gigawatts, 7 to 9 billion dollars of capex, final investment decision targeted for year-end, first power 2028, a 20-year deal with Microsoft, and the majority of generation coming from large GE Vernova turbines.
SAM: A supermajor becoming a power developer.
ALEX: That's the new margin pool. Chevron monetizes Permian gas as firm electrons, off the interconnection queue entirely, off the hyperscaler's balance sheet. And notice the ownership split emerging: in Wisconsin, We Energies wants to own the gas plants outright and socialize the cost across ratepayers. In Texas, Chevron owns and sells, keeping it off ratepayers. Same megawatts, opposite ratepayer incidence. That split — who owns the on-site plant — is going to be the whole political fight.
SAM: And all of this is being ordered against a demand curve we find out more about next week.
ALEX: Right — the hyperscaler capex prints land next Tuesday through Thursday. Meta's guiding to 125 to 145 billion this year, AWS to around 200 billion, Alphabet already printed 195 to 205 with negative free cash flow. That's the demand those 116 gigawatts of reservations and every Kilby-type campus are being built against. The question into next week is whether any of them trims the guide — because that would be the first crack in the reservation-to-firm pipeline.
